Here There Be Hordes

Game Design Document v0.26.13 2026-05-07

Contents

    1. Vision

    A real-time strategy survival game in which the player builds and defends a small industrial-era colony against waves of the infected dead. The colony grows on a true isometric grid; the dead are drawn to noise; every weapon, every building, and every wall has a different acoustic signature. You decide what to build, how loud to fight, and how long the lights stay on.

    One-line pitch: An industrial nightmare in oil paint — They Are Billions's gameplay, Risen Kingdom's brush.

    2. Design Pillars

    The following five commitments come first; everything else bends to fit them.

    Noise is the central economy of violence. Every shot, explosion, and structure makes a measurable amount of noise on a per-cell grid. Noise is what summons the horde. The player's defining tactical question is never "how much damage" — it is "how much damage for how much noise." If a feature doesn't interact with noise, we ask whether it should.

    Build, don't only fight. Most of the player's time is spent placing tents, walls, lumberyards, and power plants — not commanding squads. The colony is the protagonist; soldiers are tools that protect it. We are a base builder first and an RTS second.

    Hand-painted grim dark, mechanical depth. The art direction borrows Risen Kingdom's painterly technique and disciplined silhouettes but trades its warmth for the muted, oily, industrial-nightmare palette of They Are Billions. Visible brushwork, mean shadows, sickly light — painted horror, not pixelated horror. The mechanical systems borrow TAB's ruthlessness.

    Survival has a clock. Every mission has a known final wave. The player can see the clock ticking. Tension comes from racing the clock, not from being surprised by it.

    Failure is a teacher, not a punisher. The player can save freely (multiple slots, save anywhere). The challenge comes from the systems, not from save-scumming punishment. We trust the player to choose their own difficulty.

    3. Core Loop

    A mission plays out over a known number of in-game days. One day is roughly four to six minutes of real time at 1× speed. There is no day/night cycle in v1 — time is a single continuous counter, the way it is in They Are Billions.

    The minute-to-minute loop is: scout the surrounding cells, clear the nearby infected to make room, build economy structures (Provisions sources, Lumberyards, then Mines for Stone + Coal + Iron), upgrade housing to grow the workforce and reduce Terror, build defensive walls and towers along likely attack paths, train and position military units, weather the periodic horde attacks on milestone days, and survive to the final day. Each cycle the player gains more workers, more resources, more tech, and faces louder, denser, and more varied enemies.

    The strategic loop across a campaign is: complete a mission, unlock new units / buildings / tech / enemy types, carry that knowledge into a harder mission with new constraints, repeat for 15–30 missions.

    4. Camera, Grid, and Projection

    The world is rendered in true 2:1 isometric projection (a tile is twice as wide as it is tall). This is the They Are Billions geometry, not the gentler Risen Kingdom angle, because the iso silhouette reads better with dense crowds of small enemies and stacked vertical buildings.

    The grid is 64×32 px per tile at native resolution. This is large enough for readable hand-painted art and small enough that a colony of a few hundred buildings fits on a screen at moderate zoom. We will support three to four zoom levels (roughly 0.5×, 1.0×, 1.5×, 2.0×) and standard RTS pan controls (WASD, edge-scroll, middle-mouse drag).

    Buildings snap to the grid. Walls occupy a single tile and connect to neighbors. Units move freely between tile centers using NavigationServer2D, with the navigation mesh re-baked when walls are added or removed.

    Open question: tile size 64×32 is conservative; we could go 128×64 for higher per-tile detail at the cost of fewer tiles on screen. Locked at 64×32 for prototype, revisit when we see real art.

    5. Resource Model

    The colony runs on eight resources. Five describe physical supply, three describe colony state and capacity. Two of them are deliberate departures from genre defaults — those are the model's signatures.

    The eight resources

    Provisions — the colony's bundled lifeline: food, ammunition, clothing, lamp oil, and other daily consumables, all tracked as a single number. A unified ledger answering "do we have what we need to keep going?" Consumed by population (per worker per day), by combat (per shot, per soldier deployed), by lights and oil-burning industrial buildings (continuous drain even when idle), and by some building operations. Produced from multiple sources, each with its own cost-and-noise signature: Hunter's Cabins (early-game, quiet, low yield, distance-bound to the Command Post — useful only while the colony is small), Farms (the core of food production once you have land — see Farms below), Oil Refineries (mid-tier, consumes Coal, moderate yield + moderate noise), Munitions Workshops (mid-late, consumes Iron + Lumber, high yield, loud — the dirty bullet factory). Where your Provisions come from is itself a noise-economy decision: a colony fed by farms is quiet; a colony fed by munitions plants is a noise generator before a single soldier fires a shot. Production also rewards diversity — see Provisions Variety below.

    Lumber — cheap construction material. Low-tier walls, tents, basic buildings, Munitions Workshop input. From Lumberyards.

    Stone — defensive material. Mid-tier walls, foundations, Houses, Manors. No discrete Stone deposit on the map — Stone comes as a byproduct of any Mine working an Iron or Coal deposit (mining inevitably produces tailings/rubble; that's our Stone). One-deposit Mine gives a small Stone trickle alongside its primary output; two-deposit Mine (Iron + Coal in AOE) gives more. Industrial-era flavor + no need for a third deposit type on the map.

    Iron — late-tier industrial material. Required for Reinforced Walls, Manors, advanced towers, and Munitions Workshop production. No alloy step — Iron is collected directly from deposits via Mines, no Foundry, no Steel, no smelting. Honest extraction.

    Coal — fuel. Consumed by Coal Plants (mid/late Power) and Oil Refineries (mid Provisions). Mined.

    Workers — the population pool. Generated by housing, but housing is also one of the colony's primary Terror sinks (see Terror, below) — players upgrade housing for emotional reasons more than mechanical ones. Worker progression is deliberately modest per tier; Terror reduction is the steeper curve, and each tier also gains meaningfully more HP and defense (a Manor is a fortress in the colony's interior). Tents are free up to a soft cap (around 8) and provide minimal workers — the colony tolerates them as emergency shelter, not as a way of life. Building tents past the cap costs Lumber and adds Terror per minute (the colony objects to a permanent shantytown). Cottages (Lumber) are the floor of a real colony — paid housing, modest worker gain, the first tier that reduces Terror, and the first tier with meaningful HP. Houses (Lumber + Stone) and Manors (Stone + Iron) escalate sharply on Terror reduction, HP, and defensive armor; worker gains scale up modestly. A Manor gives roughly four times the workers of a Tent but pulls roughly four times the Terror per minute compared to a Cottage and stands up under fire that would crush a Cottage. In-place upgrades chain: Tent → Cottage → House → Manor. Workers are abstracted globally — buildings that need staff claim from the pool automatically. No micromanagement of individual peasants.

    Power — electricity. Generated by Steam Plants (early) and Coal Plants (mid-game). Required for advanced production and most defensive towers. Buildings without Power go offline by default (TAB-style brownout); a difficulty option may relax this to "reduced output" instead.

    Terror — the colony's perceived-danger scalar. Each mission starts at a base Terror level set by mission design (early-Act-1 missions might start near zero; late-Act-3 missions might start with significant baseline dread). From there, Terror responds to what the colony is and does. Rises with enemy proximity, breach events, building destruction, casualties, tent-city overcrowding (tents past the soft cap), and — importantly — rapid military attrition: losing several soldiers in a short window causes a short Terror spike (the colony watches its army die and panics; a single sniper death is grief, ten dead in a wave is collapse). Falls slowly when the colony feels safe and the things that make it safe are visible: an enclosed defensive perimeter (walls + gates with no breach paths), Provisions stable comfortably above consumption, every housing tier above Tent reducing Terror continuously (higher tier pulls harder), Provisions Variety > 1, recent milestones (research completed, day survived, wave repelled, sermon given), and a stable or growing military roster. As these conditions compound, Terror's generation rate drops below its decay rate and the meter actually walks down toward zero — a fortified, well-fed, well-housed colony with a steady army can reach near-zero Terror, briefly, between events. At low Terror the colony hums; at high Terror production slows, soldiers' accuracy drops, and panic events fire — workers desert their stations, screams add to the noise field, doors get left open. It is a narrative loss state in addition to the mechanical one. A colony can be numerically winning and emotionally collapsing.

    Provisions Variety (sub-mechanic)

    A hidden modifier called Provisions Variety counts how many distinct types of Provisions producer are currently operational in the colony. The maximum is 4 (Hunter's Cabin, Farm network, Oil Refinery, Munitions Workshop). Higher Variety produces two compounding effects: it speeds Provisions output across all producers, and it continuously suppresses Terror.

    Rough tuning targets (numbers will land in v1 implementation, not committed in this doc):

    • Variety 1: baseline production, no Terror modifier.
    • Variety 2: ~+5% production across the board, ~-1 Terror per in-game minute.
    • Variety 3: ~+10% production, ~-3 Terror per minute.
    • Variety 4: ~+15-20% production, ~-6 Terror per minute.

    Why we have this: without a diversity bonus, players would identify the most cost-efficient producer (likely Farms once they scale, or whichever path is cheapest in a given mission) and stack only that, leaving the rest unbuilt. Variety counters that pull deliberately. Players will still pick a favorite for bulk throughput — that's healthy strategy — but are incentivized to keep at least one of each producer running for the bonus. The intended outcome is a colony that looks like a diversified industrial supply chain, not a monoculture.

    Failure modes: if the bonus is too strong, every colony converges on "one of each, then max the cost-efficient one" and Variety becomes a mandatory tax. If too weak, players ignore it entirely. Tuning lands between "noticeable enough to matter" and "weak enough to skip if a producer type is cost-prohibitive in a particular mission." We will iterate in playtest.

    Signatures — what makes this model ours

    Two of the seven resources are deliberate departures from genre defaults:

    • Provisions as the colony's lifeline. Most RTS games separate food, ammunition, lighting fuel, and clothing into distinct resources. We unify them all into a single supply ledger. This abstracts away tedious accounting while preserving the principle: everything the colony does — eating, fighting, seeing in the dark — eats from the same pot. When Provisions run low, the colony is hungry AND out of ammo AND going dark, all at once. The economic strain is total; the player's question is always "what do we cut?" Combat is still economically expensive; lighting the perimeter is still a continuous drain; both compete with daily rations for the same number on the screen.
    • Terror as colony state. Turns the emotional cost of horror into mechanical pressure. The closer the dead are, the worse the colony performs. Tied directly to how safe the colony actually is right now — not to a vague mood meter.

    The model deliberately drops Gold and Steel. Gold tends to be the weakest resource in genre forerunners (TAB itself struggles to make it matter); the slot earns more for us by widening Provisions's scope. Steel was an alloy step that added a building (Foundry) and a chain without giving the player meaningful new decisions — Iron is now collected directly and used directly. Honest extraction. The Foundry is gone with it.

    Production summary

    • Provisions: Hunter's Cabin (early, quiet, low yield, must be near Command Post, falls off late-game), Farm network (Farmhouse anchor + adjacent Farmland tiles, quiet, the core mid-game food source — see §8), Oil Refinery (mid, consumes Coal, moderate yield + moderate noise), Munitions Workshop (mid-late, consumes Iron + Lumber, high yield, loud)
    • Lumber: Lumberyard
    • Coal / Iron / Stone: Mine — placed on or adjacent to Iron and/or Coal deposits in its AOE. Produces the deposit's primary output (Coal, Iron, or both); Stone always tags along as a byproduct. Two-deposit hotspots (Iron + Coal) give more Stone than single-deposit sites. A Mine with no deposits in AOE is invalid placement (won't produce anything). Site scouting is a strategic skill: a hotspot with both deposits doubles your Mine's value.
    • Workers: Tent (free up to 8, minimal workers, fragile, emergency shelter) → Cottage (Lumber, colony floor, paid, increasing HP) → House (Lumber + Stone, more HP and armor) → Manor (Stone + Iron, fortress-grade). In-place upgrades. Higher tiers exist primarily to reduce Terror and harden the colony's interior, not to grow population.
    • Power: Four sources. Steam Plant (placeable anywhere, lower output, no inputs — the fallback when a good site isn't available); Windmill (terrain-bound, must be placed on or adjacent to Hill tiles, moderate output, no inputs); Watermill (terrain-bound, must be placed adjacent to River/Water tiles, moderate-high output, no inputs); Coal Plant (mid/late, anywhere, high output, consumes Coal). Terrain-bound mills reward scouting; Steam Plant is always available as a slightly less efficient backup.
    • Terror: not produced — starts at mission baseline, accumulates from events (especially rapid military attrition), reduced by enclosed perimeter / Provisions stability / housing tiers / Provisions Variety / milestones / stable military

    6. The Noise System (Signature Feature)

    Every cell on the map carries a numerical noise value that decays toward zero over time. Events on the map deposit noise into nearby cells; off-map and idle infected periodically sample the noise field and path toward the loudest cell within their detection range. This single mechanic is the spine of the game's tension.

    Noise sources include weapon fire (per-shot or per-burst), structure operation (Munitions Workshops are loud, Coal Plants and Mines hum, Lumberyards are quiet, Farms are silent), construction (hammering walls makes noise), explosions (very loud, very wide — Bloater detonations are off-the-charts events), and enemy attacks themselves (a Brute hammering a wall draws more Brutes). Each source has an emission value and a falloff radius.

    Noise propagation is a simple radial falloff in v1 — no line-of-sight occlusion, no terrain attenuation. We can add walls-block-sound or hill-shadows in v2 if it earns its complexity cost. Decay is exponential, with a half-life of around three to five seconds depending on emitter type (gunshots decay fast, building hum is sustained).

    Weapon profiles are the player's main interaction with the noise system. The v1 roster is designed around five contrasting acoustic signatures:

    • Silenced Pistol (Scout): very low noise per shot, low damage, fast rate of fire. Anti-Light. Used for clearing isolated infected without summoning more.
    • Single-Shot Assault Rifle (Soldier): moderate noise per shot, moderate damage, fire rate similar to the silenced pistol. Generalist — okay against Light, okay against Heavy. The "I don't want to think about it" weapon.
    • Submachine Gun (Tommy Gunner): low noise per shot but very high sustained noise due to high rate of fire. Anti-Light. High DPS against Shamblers and Runners, ruinously loud. Burns Provisions fast.
    • Pump Shotgun (Shotgunner): high noise per shot, AOE cone damage, slow rate of fire. Anti-Light AOE. The panic button for breached walls.
    • Bolt-Action Rifle (Sniper): very high noise per shot, very high damage, very slow rate of fire. Anti-Heavy. One-shots Light enemies (overkill), two-shots most Heavy enemies — every shot is a horde-summoning event.

    Late-game additions (deferred to expansions) will add more profiles: Flamer (very high sustained noise, AOE burning) and Mortar (extreme noise spike at impact, devastating AOE).

    UX: the noise field should be visualizable on demand (toggle key, probably N) as a heatmap overlay. The player must be able to see what they're doing with their soundscape, not infer it from outcomes.

    7. Player Units

    The military roster is intentionally small at v1 — five combat units, all ranged, each occupying a distinct point in the noise/damage/cost space. We can add later, but every unit must earn its place; redundant overlap is the enemy of a clean roster. No melee. A close-range melee unit would push the player toward defensive postures that fight the noise mechanic; ranged-only keeps every engagement an acoustic decision.

    Each weapon has implicit effectiveness against Light vs. Heavy defensive types (see §10). Low-damage fast-firing weapons (silenced pistol, Tommy gun, shotgun) are anti-Light: they kill cleanly without overkill but deal heavily reduced damage to Heavy targets. High-damage slow-firing weapons (Sniper, Soldier rifle) are anti-Heavy or generalist: they cut through high-HP enemies efficiently but waste damage on Light targets. The Soldier rifle is the deliberate generalist — okay against everything, master of nothing, the unit you don't have to think about.

    Starting roster (Tutorial → early Act 1)

    • Scout — silenced pistol. Fast move speed, low HP, cheap. Quiet shots make the Scout the only unit who can clear isolated infected without summoning more. Used for exploration and surgical kills, not sustained firefights.
    • Soldier — single-shot assault rifle. Fire rate similar to the silenced pistol, meaningfully more damage and meaningfully more noise per shot. The workhorse of early defenses. Functional at every range, master of none.

    Unlocked through campaign (late Act 1 → Act 3)

    • Sniper — bolt-action rifle. One-shots basic enemies (Shamblers, Runners, Crawlers), two-shots most others (Brutes, Hurlers, Screamers). Slowest rate of fire in the roster; loudest weapon by per-shot noise. Built around picking targets carefully — every shot summons more.
    • Tommy Gunner — Tommy gun, full-auto. Per-shot noise is low, but sustained noise compounds fast because of the rate of fire. Burns Provisions quickly (every burst is dozens of rounds). High DPS against light enemies, devastating against runner swarms — ruinously expensive in two ways at once.
    • Shotgunner — pump shotgun, AOE cone. Lower per-shot damage than the rifle, but the AOE chews through thick waves of infected at point-blank. The wall-breach panic button.

    Late-game additions (post-v1, design-anchored but deferred to expansions): Flamer, Mortar Crew, possibly heavy-vehicle units. Brainstormed in detail when we get there.

    Healing

    Units do not heal during active combat. Wounded units recover only when they are inside the colony — within friendly territory and out of combat — at a slow passive rate. A late-tier Field Triage building accelerates recovery for any unit in its radius and consumes Provisions per healed unit per second. Severely wounded units pulled back through walls in time can be saved; units left in the field bleed out.

    Design intent: combat decisions have lasting cost. Pulling a wounded Soldier back is a real strategic choice. The player invests in a Field Triage when their roster is taking attrition faster than passive healing recovers from — usually mid-Act-2 onward.

    Target Priority modes

    Each player unit (or selected group) carries a target priority mode that dictates which enemy it auto-engages within range. Default is Closest. The player can override per-unit or per-control-group via a small HUD selector. Modes:

    • Closest — engage the nearest valid target. Default.
    • Lowest HP — finish wounded enemies first. Good for ending stalemates and clearing kill-credit.
    • Highest HP — focus the heaviest enemy first. Common Sniper choice.
    • Light only — ignore Heavy targets. Tommy Gunners and Shotgunners avoid wasting bursts on Brutes.
    • Heavy only — ignore Light targets. Snipers avoid overkill on Crawler swarms.
    • Ranged threats first — prioritize Hurlers and other ranged attackers. Counter to ranged structure threats.
    • Structure attackers first — prioritize enemies currently attacking your buildings. Defensive triage.

    Design intent: the player who manages target priority across units gets dramatically more value from their roster than one who lets everyone Closest-target. This is a depth lever for skilled play, not a new-player tax — Closest works fine for casual play, advanced modes reward attention.

    No dedicated engineer unit

    Construction and repair are abstracted, not unit-driven. Construction happens automatically once a building is placed (workers from the global pool handle it; the player never assigns an individual Engineer). Repair is a click on a damaged building — see §8.

    8. Buildings

    Buildings divide into four families: economy, housing, defense, and military production. The list below is the v1 target, not exhaustive.

    Economy: Hunter's Cabin (early Provisions, quiet, low yield, must be placed within ~12 tiles of the Command Post; effectively obsolete once the colony scales past mid-Act-1), Farmhouse + Farmland (the core Provisions network — see below), Oil Refinery (mid Provisions, consumes Coal, moderate noise), Munitions Workshop (late Provisions, consumes Iron + Lumber, loud), Lumberyard (Lumber; terrain-bound to Forest tiles — must be placed on or adjacent to a Forest), Mine (Coal / Iron + Stone as byproduct; terrain-bound to Iron and/or Coal deposit tiles in AOE — invalid placement if neither is present), Steam Plant (early Power, placeable anywhere, lower yield), Windmill (early Power, terrain-bound to Hill tiles, moderate yield), Watermill (early Power, terrain-bound to River/Water tiles, moderate-high yield), Coal Plant (mid/late Power, anywhere, consumes Coal, high yield).

    Farms (network mechanic): Farms operate as networks. The player first places a Farmhouse, the network anchor. Adjacent open cells can then be developed into Farmland — small, cheap individual tiles that produce Provisions when continuously connected back to a Farmhouse. Farmland chains as the player keeps adding adjacent tiles, so a colony's farms sprawl outward from each Farmhouse. If a Farmhouse is destroyed and no other Farmhouse remains in that connected network, all the network's Farmland goes inert until rebuilt. Players are explicitly encouraged to maintain at least two Farmhouses per major network — one always-on, one for redundancy. Multiple separate networks are fine and often strategic (split your supply across the map).

    Housing: Tent (free up to 8, ~2 workers each, fragile — past the cap each tent costs Lumber and adds Terror); Cottage (Lumber, ~4 workers, first tier to reduce Terror, modest HP); House (Lumber + Stone, ~6 workers, medium Terror reduction, real HP and basic armor); Manor (Stone + Iron, ~8 workers, strongest Terror reduction, fortress-grade HP and armor). Each upgrade replaces the previous in place; the chain is Tent → Cottage → House → Manor. The primary reason to upgrade past Cottage is Terror reduction and interior hardening, not population.

    Defense: Wood Wall (cheap, low HP), Stone Wall (Stone, mid HP), Reinforced Wall (Stone + Iron, high HP), Gate (passable for player, blocks enemies), Wood Watchtower (basic ranged garrison), Stone Watchtower (longer range, more HP), Ballista (heavy single-target anti-armor; slow rate of fire, big damage per shot — counters Heavy enemies), Mortar Tower (AOE artillery; lobbed shells with arc trajectory; very loud), Repeater (sustained-fire anti-swarm; period machine-gun emplacement; fast rate, lower per-shot damage; best vs. Light swarms; continuous noise). Floodlight Tower dropped per v0.19.0 — no clear gameplay role.

    Support: Field Triage (accelerates unit healing within radius; consumes Provisions per healed unit per second; the player's answer to attrition once roster losses outpace passive in-base recovery).

    Military Production: Barracks trains every v1 military unit — Scout, Soldier, Tommy Gunner, Shotgunner, Sniper. The previously-planned Workshop is dropped (Barracks does its job). A future Armory / Factory production building is planned post-v1 to handle Steam- and Coal-powered units (Flamer first, possibly heavy-vehicle units), but doesn't ship in v1. Research Lab (research/tech tree access; not a unit-producing building; requires ~8 workers to operate — the colony's most worker-intensive single building, an investment that genuinely costs you population for the duration). Command Post (the colony's heart — destroyed = mission failed).

    Operations: Repair

    Damaged buildings can be repaired by selecting them and choosing Repair. Cost: roughly 25% of the building's original construction cost, paid in the original materials. Requires no enemies within ~6 tiles (the colony cannot work under fire). Repair takes time proportional to the damage taken — full repair from near-destroyed is several seconds. Designed to give the player a meaningful post-attack recovery loop without requiring a dedicated repair unit.

    The HUD also offers a Repair All button — single click, the colony pays to repair every damaged building it currently can (each subject to the same no-enemies-nearby rule, so contested buildings are skipped). Insufficient resources? It repairs as many as it can afford in priority order (defenses first, then production, then housing). The button preview shows total cost before committing. Standard expectation post-wave: scan the map, click Repair All, get back to building.

    Construction works the same way: placing a building queues construction; workers from the global pool handle it automatically. The player never assigns or commands an individual builder.

    9. Tech Tree

    A small, branching research tree gated behind the Research Lab. Three branches:

    Industry — economic upgrades (faster gather rates, larger storage, cheaper housing, cheaper energy).

    Arms — military upgrades (unit damage, unit HP, unlock new units, ammo capacity).

    Walls — defensive upgrades (wall HP, tower range, tower fire rate, gate HP, cheaper construction).

    Each branch has 4–6 nodes in v1, of which most missions allow unlocking two to three. Tech research costs Iron (with smaller Provisions costs for sustained late-tier projects) and takes real-time to complete — a research takes 30–90 seconds at 1× depending on tier. Researches do not persist across missions; each mission's tech is its own arc, set by mission design.

    10. Enemy Roster

    The infected come in five v1 archetypes plus late-campaign variants. All are drawn by noise; their differences are how they behave when they arrive — and crucially, which player weapons can handle them via the Light/Heavy defensive type system.

    Defensive types: Light vs. Heavy

    Every enemy is one of two defensive types. This is not "armor" — it's a flavor of resilience that creates a rock-paper-scissors matchup with the player's weapon roster.

    • Light — low HP, often fast or numerous. Dies quickly to almost anything. But high-damage weapons waste damage on them — a Sniper one-shotting a Crawler is the same effective output as ten Crawlers absorbed. Best handled by low-damage, fast-firing, or AOE weapons (silenced pistol, Tommy gun, shotgun).
    • Heavy — high HP, slower, harder-to-kill flesh. Low-damage weapons deal dramatically reduced damage per shot to Heavy enemies (a Tommy gun chewing on a Brute is doing pity damage even though it's hitting). Best handled by high-damage per-shot weapons (Sniper, Soldier rifle).

    Design intent: a balanced army is the strongest army. Pure Snipers struggle when the wave is Crawler swarms; pure Tommy Gunners die to Brutes. Players who maintain a varied roster — Sniper for Heavy, Tommy/Shotgun for Light, Soldier rifles as flex — clear waves cleanly. Combined with Target Priority modes (§7), this is the depth-side of combat play.

    v1 archetypes

    • Shambler [Light] — slow, weak, melee. Cannon fodder. The archetype most easily handled by walls + basic towers.
    • Runner [Light] — fast, weak, melee. Punishes wall gaps and anyone caught in the open. The reason Shotgunners exist.
    • Screamer [Light] — fragile, melee, but on death or alert deposits a large noise pulse. Priority kill target — and the player's tradeoff is that killing it loudly draws more horde than letting it die quietly.
    • Brute [Heavy] — slow, very high HP, heavy melee. Specifically targets walls and structures. The reason Reinforced Walls exist; the reason Snipers earn their cost.
    • Hurler [Heavy] — slow, medium HP, ranged: throws chunks of bone and debris at structures from beyond wall range. Replaces the originally-planned "Spitter" archetype with cleaner industrial-era flavor (the body has hardened after death, fragments come off as projectiles). Drives Sniper investment as the answer to ranged enemy threats outside wall range.

    Late-campaign archetypes

    • Crawler [Light] — very small, very fast, low HP. Single shots kill, but the small profile is easy to overlook in mixed waves while bigger threats demand the player's focus. Chips at walls and weakened units while the player is busy elsewhere.
    • Bloater [Heavy] — bloated, suicidal infected that detonates on contact with structures or massed units, dealing massive AOE damage. Faster than a Brute. Exists specifically to make walls insufficient as a sole defense — a Bloater's explosion will breach a Wood Wall outright and meaningfully damage a Stone Wall, demanding an active military response. Replaces the originally-planned "Harpy" archetype (wall-jumper) with a more grounded, more horror-flavored solution to the same design problem: walls cannot be everything.
    • Giant [Heavy] — larger and more dangerous than the Brute. Mid-late wave appearance on harder maps. Very high HP, very high damage, slow. Anti-structure focus — walks through low-tier walls, stalls on Reinforced Walls. Distinct from Boss-class enemies: Giants are a regular enemy class that can appear multiple times in a wave; Bosses are scripted one-off mission encounters. (added v0.19.0)
    • Boss-class [Heavy] — mission-specific finale enemies. Giant zombies (TAB-style) are the reference: massive HP pools, slow movement, devastating attacks, often unique mechanics per encounter. One or two per finale, with careful telegraphing.

    Most missions deploy a subset of the roster, not the full set. The campaign introduces archetypes one at a time so the player has time to learn each before they stack.

    11. Wave System

    A mission's enemy schedule has three layers stacked on top of each other.

    Ambient pressure — small numbers of infected wandering near the player's noise field, attracted by ambient base operation. Constant low-level threat.

    Pulse waves — scheduled mini-waves on regular cadence (every two days, say), small to medium size, from one or two directions. These are the rhythm of the mission, the test of whether the player's economy and defense are keeping pace.

    Horde days — milestone days (5, 10, 15, ..., final) bring a major attack from a known direction (telegraphed in advance, ideally with an arrow on the minimap). The horde grows with the day number and includes a wider variety of archetypes. The final day is the largest horde, attacking from all directions simultaneously.

    The wave director composes each wave from the mission's allowed archetype set, scaling counts to the current day and to the player's "noise budget" — louder colonies face larger waves, by design.

    12. Mission Structure & Campaign Sketch

    The campaign comprises 15–30 hand-authored missions. Most are fixed-day defenses (the spine), with about 25–30% being objective-based variants (the spice). Each mission introduces, at most, one new unit, one new building, or one new enemy archetype — the player should never face two unfamiliar things at once.

    Approximate campaign arc

    • Tutorial (3 missions) — introduce build, defense, and the starting unit roster (Scout, Soldier). Mission 3 ends in a small "horde day" to teach wave defense.
    • Act 1 — Outbreak (5 missions) — fixed-day defenses on small maps. Introduces the noise system, walls, towers, Shamblers, Runners, Tommy Gunner, and the first horde day milestone. Player faces increasing day counts (10 → 15 → 20).
    • Act 2 — Fortify (6–8 missions) — mid-game. Introduces the Mine (Stone / Coal / Iron extraction), Coal Plants, Oil Refineries (additional Provisions path), Munitions Workshops, the Manor housing tier, Brutes, Hurlers, the Sniper (the answer to Hurler ranged attacks), and the Shotgunner (the answer to Runner swarms). First objective-based mission (e.g. extract a survivor from a contaminated zone). First mission with multiple defense fronts.
    • Act 3 — Reclaim (6–10 missions) — late-game. Introduces Screamers, Crawlers, Bloaters (the suicide-infected answer to "walls alone aren't enough"), boss-class enemies; Ballistae / Mortar Towers / Repeaters and Reinforced Walls become available; Field Triage appears for roster sustainability. Finale is a long mission with a multi-front final assault and a unique objective beyond survival.

    Persistent-map mechanics (resources or tech carrying between missions) appear in late Act 2 and Act 3 in limited form — for example, two consecutive missions on the same colony, or a mission where you start with a tech your previous mission unlocked. Full TAB-style persistent world map is not in scope for v1.

    13. Pacing & Time Controls

    Real-time-with-pause, TAB-style. Space toggles pause; the player can issue and queue orders while paused. Game speed selector with four settings (0.5×, 1×, 2×, 4×) on number-row keys 1–4, defaulting to 1×. The pause UI clearly indicates "PAUSED" so the player never confuses a stuck game for a paused one.

    Auto-pause triggers (configurable in settings): on enemy unit entering vision, on building destruction, on horde-day announcement, on resource shortfall.

    14. Save System

    Save anywhere, multiple named slots per mission, plus rolling autosaves (at minimum: every two in-game days, on horde-day milestones, on quit). The save format is Godot's native binary Resource saver targeting a per-mission SaveGame.tres. Multiple slots are stored as separate files; the player names them. Each slot remembers its mission, day, difficulty, and a brief screenshot.

    We deliberately reject the TAB single-save-slot model. The user picked save-anywhere; the design accommodates it without apology.

    15. Art Direction

    Hand-painted grim dark horror — Risen Kingdom's painterly technique applied to They Are Billions's industrial nightmare. Visible oil-painted brushwork. A muted palette pulled toward soot, rust, mossy greens, sickly yellows, and the cold blues of fog and night. Warm color exists only where light sources put it — gas lamps, oil flames, weapon muzzle flashes — small pools of safety surrounded by deep, mean shadow. No saturated daylight. No friendly skies.

    Risen Kingdom's silhouette discipline survives. Buildings and units must read at a glance, even at moderate zoom. What changes is the affection inside that silhouette: it belongs to Goya, not a children's book. Oil-paint dread, not pixel-art dread.

    The infected are visibly diseased and visibly suffering. Horror lives in the beat between revulsion and pity, never in gore-pornography. Buildings show their decay: rotted wood, exposed pipes, soot-stained walls, smokestacks bleeding into a permanently overcast sky. The colony is the only candle in a graveyard.

    The industrial-era frame is deliberately fuzzy in v0.2: Victorian-steampunk, early-industrial, and dieselpunk are all viable skins for the painted style. What is committed: gas lamps, oil flames, and electric arcs as lighting sources, set against a permanently overcast/twilight ambient. Era-specific commitments wait until commissioned art arrives.

    Asset strategy for prototype: placeholder colored isometric tiles + free isometric assets from Kenney, OpenGameArt, or itch.io packs for early prototyping, with a clear plan to replace placeholders with commissioned art once the core loop is validated. We will not ship with placeholders.

    UI direction: rusted-iron-and-blood-stained-parchment diegetic frames. The HUD should feel like a frantic clerk's last report from a city already half-lost, not a sci-fi command console.

    16. Technical Plan

    Engine: Godot 4.6.2 (locked as of 2026-05-06). Project's config/features is set to "4.6". Bumping to a newer 4.x point release is fine; jumping to 5.x will require a migration pass.

    Language: GDScript primary. C# as a fallback only if profiling shows GDScript bottlenecks we can't otherwise resolve. Most TAB-style perf concerns (hundreds of pathfinding agents) are addressable with sensible architecture before we reach for C#.

    Pathfinding: NavigationServer2D with a tile-snapped nav mesh. Walls and gates rebake the affected nav region. For dense crowds, we'll evaluate flow-field pathfinding as a supplement — TAB famously uses flow fields for the horde, A* for individual player units, and we'll likely want the same hybrid.

    Save format: Godot binary Resource (.tres/.res). Multi-slot, per-mission.

    Rendering: 2D, isometric. Y-sorting on a single CanvasLayer. Tile size 64×32. Native resolution 1920×1080, with viewport stretch mode and integer scaling.

    Architecture sketch

    • source/autoloads/ — global singletons (Constants, GameState, NoiseField, WaveDirector, EventBus)
    • source/ui/ — main menu, HUD, in-game panels
    • source/world/ — tile grid, terrain, lighting
    • source/buildings/ — building scenes, base classes, families
    • source/units/ — unit scenes, base classes, AI controllers
    • source/enemies/ — enemy archetypes, AI, the wave director
    • source/missions/ — per-mission scenes and configs
    • source/save/ — save-load infrastructure
    • assets/ — art, audio, fonts
    • tests/ — gut tests for non-trivial systems

    Source control: GitHub, single repository, master/main branch + feature branches for major systems.

    17. Scope & Non-goals (v1)

    • Single player only. No multiplayer of any kind.
    • No skirmish AI — only wave-based threats, no opposing-faction commander.
    • Hand-authored missions only — no procedural mission generation in v1.
    • English only — but all player-facing strings route through tr() from day one so localization is a build-time problem, not a refactor.
    • Mouse + keyboard primary — controller support is post-v1.
    • No mod support in v1, but the data-driven mission/unit/building format should make it cheap to add later.
    • No persistent world map — explicitly deferred to a v2 if v1 lands.
    • No campaign-wide persistent unlocks beyond what each mission's design hands the player at start.

    Camera projection: true 2:1 isometric2026-05-05

    TAB-style geometry, not Risen Kingdom's gentler axonometric tilt. Reads better with dense crowds and stacked buildings. (§4)

    Setting: industrial era, infected/zombie horde2026-05-05

    Specific era (Victorian / dieselpunk / generic) deliberately deferred until art is committed. Open to steampunk if assets fit. (§15)

    Art tone: hand-painted grim dark horror2026-05-05

    Risen Kingdom's painterly technique and silhouette discipline applied to They Are Billions's industrial nightmare. Muted palette pulled toward soot/rust/sickly green/cold blue; warmth only from in-fiction light sources (lamps, fire, muzzle flashes) against mean shadow. Painted horror, not pixelated horror. Replaced earlier "hand-painted warmth" direction. (§2 Pillar 3, §15)

    Pacing: real-time with smart pause + speed controls2026-05-05

    Spacebar pause with order queueing while paused. Speed selector at 0.5× / 1× / 2× / 4×. (§13)

    Save model: save anywhere, multiple slots2026-05-05

    Explicitly rejected the TAB hardcore single-save model. The challenge comes from systems, not from save-scumming punishment. (§14)

    Mission shape: mixed, fixed-day defense as backbone2026-05-05

    Most missions are fixed-day defenses. Objective specials (~25–30%) introduce new units/buildings/enemies one at a time. Limited persistent-map elements appear in late Act 2 and Act 3. (§12)

    Resource model v2: 8 resources, 2 signatures, no alloys2026-05-05

    Provisions, Lumber, Stone, Iron, Coal, Workers, Power, Terror. Two signatures: Provisions bundles food/ammo/clothing/lamp-oil/consumables into one unified supply ledger (lights, combat, and rations all consume the same pool); Terror tracks colony safety as a mechanical pressure. Drops Gold. Drops Steel and the Foundry — Iron is collected directly from deposits, no alloy step. Lamp Oil folded into Provisions. Replaces earlier "7 resources, Steel-as-alloy" formulation; the alloy step added a building chain without giving the player meaningful new decisions. Iron and Coal are now player-facing pools, both extracted via the unified Mine building. (§5)

    Provisions Variety bonus2026-05-05

    A hidden modifier counts the distinct active Provisions producer types (max 4: Hunter's Camp, Farm, Oil Refinery, Munitions Workshop). Higher Variety speeds production across all producers AND continuously reduces Terror. Rough tuning: +5/+10/+15-20% production at Variety 2/3/4; -1/-3/-6 Terror per minute at Variety 2/3/4. Counters the "one efficient producer monoculture" failure mode while still letting players pick a bulk-throughput favorite. Tuning iterates in playtest. (§5)

    Housing economy: Terror sink + interior fortress, worker pump third2026-05-05

    Tents are free up to a soft cap (~8); past the cap each tent costs Lumber and adds Terror per minute. Cottages (Lumber) are the floor of a real colony. Chain: Tent → Cottage → House → Manor (renamed from "Stone House"). Each tier provides modestly more workers, significantly more Terror reduction, AND meaningfully more HP and defense — a Manor is a fortress at the colony's heart. Manor cost is Stone + Iron (Steel-as-alloy was dropped — see Resource model v2). Players upgrade housing for Terror reduction and interior hardening before population growth. (§5, §8)

    Military production unified at Barracks (Workshop / Armory deferred)2026-05-06

    Per v0.19.0: Barracks trains every v1 military unit (Scout, Soldier, Tommy Gunner, Shotgunner, Sniper). The previously-planned Workshop is dropped — Barracks does its role. The previously-planned Armory (Sniper + late-game heavy weapons) is also retired in v1 form. A future Armory or Factory production building is planned post-v1 to handle Steam- and Coal-powered units (Flamer first, possibly heavy-vehicle units), but doesn't ship at v1. Replaces the prior "Armory = Sniper-tier" decision. (§8, C-13)

    Unit roster v1: 5 ranged combat units, no Engineer, no melee2026-05-05

    Roster: Scout (silenced pistol), Soldier (single-shot assault rifle), Sniper (bolt-action), Tommy Gunner (full-auto SMG), Shotgunner (pump-action AOE). Starting two are Scout + Soldier; Sniper / Tommy / Shotgun unlock through campaign progression. The previously-planned Engineer unit is dropped — a low-tier melee unit fights the noise-mechanic identity, and construction/repair are abstracted (§8 Operations: Repair). Late-game additions (Flamer, Mortar) deferred to post-v1. (§7)

    Repair as click-to-repair, no Engineer needed2026-05-05

    Damaged buildings repaired by selection + Repair button. Cost ~25% of original construction cost in original materials. Requires no enemies within ~6 tiles. Time proportional to damage taken. Construction follows the same abstracted model — placement queues workers from the global pool, no per-unit assignment. (§8)

    Healing: in-base passive + Field Triage acceleration2026-05-05

    Units only heal when inside friendly colony territory AND out of combat. Default passive rate is slow — pulling a wounded unit back is a real tactical choice. A Field Triage building (late-tier Support) accelerates healing within its radius, consuming Provisions per healed unit per second. Units left wounded in the field bleed out. (§7 Healing)

    Farm system: Farmhouse anchor + Farmland network2026-05-05

    Farms operate as networks. The player places a Farmhouse (anchor); adjacent open cells can then be developed into Farmland tiles that produce Provisions when continuously connected back to a Farmhouse. Farmland chains as the player keeps adding adjacent tiles. If a Farmhouse is destroyed and no other Farmhouse remains in that connected network, all the network's Farmland goes inert until rebuilt — players are encouraged to keep at least two Farmhouses per major network for redundancy.     Light vs. Heavy defensive types2026-05-05

    Every enemy is one of two defensive types: Light or Heavy. Not armor — a flavor of resilience that creates a rock-paper-scissors matchup with the player's weapon roster. Light enemies die quickly but high-damage weapons waste damage on them (overkill). Heavy enemies dramatically reduce damage from low-damage weapons. A balanced army is the strongest army; specialized rosters fail in mixed waves. The Soldier rifle is the deliberate generalist. (§10, §7)

    Difficulty: % multiplier with named tiers2026-05-06

    Forest density target on Easy / forest maps: 30%2026-05-06

    Forest / Easy mission maps target 30% live tree coverage. Locked in v0.16.2 after the first F5 review of the v0.16.1 reachability gap-fill — Nick: "the tree density is ideal." 30% gives enough cover to narrow horde paths and create natural choke points without caging the player or wasting most of the map on impassable terrain. Initial design called for ~50%; the iteration found that lower density reads better and plays better. Clusters are organic blobs (40-90 tiles each, BFS-expanded with 0.65 probability per neighbor). Generation drops clusters until coverage hits the target, then runs the reachability-flood gap-fill pass. Per-mission Resource (S-17) overrides the default; harder mission biomes use lower coverage and / or dead trees. (gameplay.gd FOREST_COVERAGE_TARGET = 0.30)

    Forest gap-fill: no enclosed grass pockets, any size2026-05-06

    After clustering, the gap-fill pass floods 4-connected from a known-walkable seed (the unit's start tile / future Command Post) across passable grass; any grass tile NOT reached is part of an enclosed pocket and gets converted to a tree. Catches enclosed pockets of any size or shape — single-cell pinholes, 2x2 pockets, large irregular dead zones. (Earlier v0.15.0 implementation only handled single-cell holes; v0.16.1 corrected this after Nick flagged that larger gaps were slipping through.) 4-connected flood matches the unit's actual reachability under our A* (DIAGONAL_MODE_ONLY_IF_NO_OBSTACLES): if the flood says a cell is unreachable, the A* would say so too. Visual rule: no marooned grass islands inside a forest mass. Players still get the gaps between separate clusters that connect to the rest of the map; what they don't get is enclosed pockets they can't reach. IsoGrid.gap_fill_forests(seed, fill_type). (S-30)

    Pathfinding: AStarGrid2D, 4-conn + diagonal-no-corner-cutting2026-05-06

    All unit movement goes through real A* pathfinding via Godot's built-in AStarGrid2D. IsoGrid.rebuild_pathing() bakes the impassability map after generation; IsoGrid.find_path(start, goal) wraps get_id_path(allow_partial_path=true) so a click on a forest tile naturally routes the unit to the nearest reachable tile next to it, and a click across a forest finds the shortest walkable route around it. Diagonal moves are allowed only when both adjacent cardinals are clear (DIAGONAL_MODE_ONLY_IF_NO_OBSTACLES) — units can't slip through the corner between two diagonal trees. Units consume returned tile paths as ordered screen-space waypoints (unit.move_along_path()). On any future impassable terrain change (tree harvest, wall placed/destroyed, building destroyed), the call site is responsible for invoking IsoGrid.rebuild_pathing(). Production replacement when crowd sizes climb past ~200 enemies: NavigationServer2D / flow-field for hordes (S-08). (S-07)

    Lumberyard AOE non-overlap2026-05-06

    Two Lumberyards cannot have overlapping 5x5 work areas. A new Lumberyard's candidate tile must be at Chebyshev distance ≥ 2*RANGE + 1 (= 5 with RANGE=2) from every existing Lumberyard so the AOEs don't share a single cell. Site selection becomes a real choice — the player has to think about where the next Lumberyard goes, not just spam them across one forest. Implementation lives in lumberyard.is_valid_placement() via _overlaps_existing_lumberyard(); the ghost preview turns red when the rule fires so the constraint is visible during placement. Same non-overlap rule will likely apply to the Mine when it lands (S-24), since deposit AOE behavior is parallel. (S-03)

    Dead-tree variant for harder maps (impassable, still harvestable)2026-05-06

    Harder mission types use DEAD_FOREST tiles (greyed-out, blighted pines) at lower coverage than the Forest/Easy default. Dead trees stay impassable AND still produce Lumber when in range of a Lumberyard — they're a visual variant that explains why a Wasteland or Desert map has fewer trees on it (in-fiction: blight, fire, time), not a "no lumber" mechanic. The harder-map Lumber pinch comes from reduced coverage and harder placement, not from poisoned-output tiles. Tile type lives in IsoGrid.TileType.DEAD_FOREST; renderer uses the existing pine sprite with a desaturated modulate until commissioned dead-tree art lands; Lumberyard counts FOREST + DEAD_FOREST in its AOE. (M-01, S-31)
